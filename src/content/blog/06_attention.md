I was lying on the couch at noon listening to a podcast, probably something about Python. Suddenly a message came in, I had intended to keep listening while reading that message, but quickly realized the sound had turned into white noise, a constant buzzing, and I couldn't concentrate on a single word. That's because I had the message in front of my eye, and my eyes were glued to the text.

I tried several times. Every time my attention turned to the screen, the sound in my ears lost its meaning. There was no transition; it degenerated directly from "language" into "acoustic signal." The sound was still coming in, my ears were fine, but it seemed that the layer of processing where the sound was translated into meaning had broken down.

Cognitive science calls this dual-task interference. Reading and listening to audio both require semantic processing, which uses the same pathway. Baddeley's working memory model has a component called the phonological loop. When two tasks compete for this loop simultaneously, one will inevitably be suppressed. Verification is simple: reading while listening to instrumental music is almost unaffected because the music uses a different system. Switching to songs with lyrics immediately brings back the interference.

Some say it's possible to multitask. Yes, but with conditions. Cognitive science distinguishes between two processing modes: automatic processing and controlled processing. Automatic processing consumes almost no attentional resources, while controlled processing requires them. The premise for multitasking to be possible is that at least one task is already automated. Walking is automated, so you can listen to a podcast while walking. Experienced drivers can have deep conversations on familiar routes because the driving part has been degraded to automatic operation. Novices cannot do this. The truth about multitasking is: one task no longer requires mental effort; the mind is only focused on the other.

Reading and listening to the radio are both highly semantically demanding, controllable tasks. There's no possibility of multitasking between these two activities; the only possibility is rapid switching. However, the high content density of radio broadcasts means that even a slight shift away from the context leads to losing track of the original message, and switching back results in being unable to catch up. Thus, it becomes a constant buzzing.

Later, I am done responding to the mesaage and lay down to listen attentively. But I quickly started drifting towards sleep. The sound remained the same, the sound was still on, and the podcast turned into a buzzing sound again. This time, the mechanism is different. Reading involves two tasks competing for one channel. Falling asleep involves the channel itself closing. When the brain transitions from wakefulness to sleep, semantic decoding is among the first advanced functions to be shut down. However, the auditory cortex shuts down much later, so sound signals are still coming in, but the module that translates sound into meaning has already gone offline. I say, "My intention is to listen," but willpower doesn't work here. Wanting to listen is useless. The module responsible for "understanding" is no longer under conscious control. It follows the state of consciousness; as consciousness sinks, it retreats.

Looking at the two observations side-by-side, I noticed an interesting characteristic of attention. It has its own preferences: when the eyes scan the text, they are drawn in without me having to make a decision. It has its own stamina cycle: when it gets tired, it retreats on its own, and I can't stop it. It can only stay in one place at a time. It can be guided, but it cannot be forced.

Then I noticed something else. When attention is racing through the mind, it moves incredibly fast. One thought triggers the next, almost without friction. It jumps from idea to memory to image to judgment, without any external constraints. But when reading, it slows down. Its speed doesn't change, but the text limits it. A book is linear, word after word, line after line; even the fastest attention can only move along the track. And when it's locked onto that narrow track by the text, all its mobility is consumed in moving along the text, leaving no room to process another flow of information. This also explains why headphones become a buzzing sound when reading. Conversely, when attention is freely racing through the mind, the freedom to switch between ideas is much greater.

It's amazing how it resembles the founding paper of modern large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini - "Attention is all you need". Attention is anchered at each word, linearly, one at a time, with the information going through as what am I looking for (Q)
What do I mean (K), and What information do I actually hold (V)? The mind is also narrowed when you are reading or listening to a podcast, one word at a time, trying to understand its meaning (K), where does it fit with what I have read (Q), and what does it add to the story (V).

What if we didn't read? Would the human brain be more powerful? Would it be closer to some primitive state? 

Humans have had written language for about five thousand years, but widespread literacy has only existed for a few hundred years. Evolution didn't have time to develop new brain regions specifically for reading. The brain did one thing: requisition. Stanislas Dehaene calls this neuronal recycling. Reading requisitions a region of the ventral occipital-temporal cortex, which was originally responsible for object recognition, face recognition, and visual pattern recognition in the environment. After learning to read, it was transformed into the Visual Word Form Area, specifically for processing text.

Reading is far more than just a skill. It literally transforms the physical structure of the brain.

Let's return to attention. The attention pattern developed through reading is linear, sequential, and narrowly focused. People who read extensively over a long period may have their default attentional pattern shaped into a more linear one. Attention, unshaped by reading, is more like a wide-angle lens, receiving a large amount of information simultaneously, scanning rapidly, without needing to lock onto a single line. People in oral cultures don't read books, yet they can recite entire epics, navigate by footprints and celestial phenomena, and simultaneously extract a large amount of non-textual signals from their environment. That's a different kind of brain.

Growing up, I always like reading, I enjoy getting informaiton from words. I even prefer reading over watching a video sometimes for the same information, as I can control the speed. Whereas looking at my kid, who probably would be considered a ADHD (or just a boy) by morden standards, doesn't like any reading. But on the other hand he has way better spacial sense than me, and can just learn to solve a rubic cube so naturally.

It seems like the brain retained that functional area for its orignal purpose. And we lebal them ADHD.

For people who meditate, they always describe the experience as consciousness expanding or dreamlike flow-state. The sensory system can enter in place without a frame of reference or an object to ancher, it gets in touch with the nature. A brain will go from hyper focused to full relaxition, just like relexing muscles.

This is completely different from the attention we observed earlier. Attention is always directed at something—a book, a sound, a thought; its behavior is all revealed in the interaction with the object. In that childhood experience, the object was removed. The characteristics of attention—preferences, speed, being throttled by text, being shut down by sleep—all occur at the level of its interaction with content. The toy we played with as children is beneath the content, at a deeper level than attention itself. Attention is merely one application of it.

Children's brains are highly flexible, with less rigid isolation between neural networks and far more connections between regions than adults. This loose connection structure may be the hardware foundation for that experience. As the brain matures, specializes, and pathways solidify, the degrees of freedom narrow. The adult brain is more efficient, but this efficiency comes at the cost of greater rigidity.

The process of that ability disappearing was probably gradual, so slow that you wouldn't notice it at the time. It's like the semantic channel closing when you fall asleep; you don't feel it, only later discovering that the sound has turned into a buzzing. The door to childhood might have closed in the same way. There wasn't a specific moment; you just realized one day that you could never go back in.
