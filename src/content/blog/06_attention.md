I was taking a walk and listening to a podcast when a message came in. I meant to keep listening while I read it, but within seconds the audio had dissolved into white noise: a constant, meaningless hum. My eyes were on the text, and the part of my mind that turns sound into language had quietly gone offline.

I tried a few more times. Every time my attention moved to the screen, the podcast stopped being speech. It wasn't that I stopped hearing; the sound was still arriving. It was as if the layer that translates audio signal into meaning had been temporarily vacated.

Cognitive science has a name for this: attentional bottleneck. Reading and listening to speech both route through the same semantic processing channel. In Baddeley's model of working memory, this channel is called the phonological loop, the system that holds and manipulates language-based information. Two tasks competing for it simultaneously means one gets starved. The simplest verification is to swap the podcast for instrumental music: the interference mostly disappears, because wordless music draws on a different system. Add lyrics back, and it returns immediately.

Stanislas Dehaene, in *Consciousness and the Brain*, offers a deeper account of why. His global workspace theory describes consciousness as a kind of central broadcast station: information becomes conscious when it achieves sufficient amplification to ignite a network spanning the frontal and parietal cortex, broadcasting a single signal across the whole brain at once. That station has only one channel. Two competing streams do not share it; they fight over it. What I was experiencing during that walk was not a failure of effort but an architectural constraint. The workspace was occupied.

This points to something worth distinguishing: not all multitasking is impossible, just certain kinds. Cognitive scientists separate automatic processing from controlled processing. Automatic tasks have been practiced into reflex and run in the background without demanding attention. Controlled tasks require it. You can walk and listen to a podcast because walking, for most adults, has become automatic. However, listening to a dense podcast while reading is asking two controlled tasks to share one channel. There is no way to truly do both simultaneously, only to switch rapidly. And because podcast speech arrives at a fixed rate, any gap in attention means losing context, and reentry is disorienting. The whole thing collapses into buzzing.

## The Narrow Track

Later, I came home from my walk, laid down on the couch, and continued to listen to my podcast. But I started drifting toward sleep. The podcast turned into buzzing again. Same sound, it seemed, same problem. But the mechanism was different. This time, it wasn't two tasks competing for one channel. The channel itself was closing. As the brain transitions out of wakefulness, semantic decoding is among the first higher functions to shut down. The auditory cortex stays active much longer, so sound keeps arriving, but the module that converts it into meaning has already gone offline. No amount of wanting to stay awake helped. Willpower doesn't reach that deep. The understanding module doesn't take instructions from consciousness; it follows the state of consciousness. As that state sinks, the module retreats with it.

Looking at both experiences together, I started noticing some consistent properties of attention. It has preference: when my eyes landed on the text, attention was pulled in without any decision on my part. It has a stamina cycle. When it tires, it retreats on its own. It can only occupy one place at a time. It can be guided, but not forced.

Then I noticed something about its speed. When attention is left unanchored, when the mind is just running freely, it moves fast. One thought sparks the next with almost no friction: idea to memory to image to judgment, flowing without external constraint. But reading slows it down. Not the speed exactly, since attention still moves at the same rate, but the text constrains it. A book is a narrow track: word after word, line after line. Even the fastest attention can only follow the sequence. Locked onto that track, all of attention's mobility is consumed just moving forward through the words. There's nothing left to route toward a simultaneous audio stream. That's why the podcast turns to noise.

## A Familiar Architecture

This is, it turns out, precisely how attention works inside large language models like GPT, Claude, and Gemini. The founding paper is literally called *Attention Is All You Need*. In transformer models, attention is also anchored word by word, moving through a sequence. At every position, the model asks three questions: what am I looking for (Q, the query), what does each word signal about itself (K, the key), and what does each word actually carry (V, the value)? The mechanism determines which values to pull based on how well each key matches the query. Reading works the same way, moving through text one at a time, asking what do we know about the story so far (Q), what does the word mean in context (K), and what does it add to the story (V). The architecture of reading and the architecture of transformer attention are solving the same problem.

Which raises a strange question: what would human cognition look like without reading?

## What Reading Does to the Brain

Written language has existed for roughly five thousand years. Widespread literacy is far more recent, a few centuries at most. Evolution doesn't work on that timescale. The brain wasn't redesigned for reading; it improvised. In *Reading in the Brain*, Dehaene calls this neuronal recycling: the brain requisitions existing circuitry for a new purpose. A region of the ventral occipitotemporal cortex, originally used for recognizing objects, faces, and environmental patterns, gets repurposed into what Dehaene calls the Visual Word Form Area, specialized for processing written text. What makes this remarkable is its universality: the same region activates across readers of radically different writing systems, French, Hebrew, Chinese, and others. The brain did not evolve separate solutions for each script but found a single piece of circuitry suited to detecting the angles, junctions, and arrangements that written symbols share across cultures.

Reading doesn't just train a skill. It physically reorganizes the brain.

And the reorganization runs deep. The attention pattern that reading instills is linear, sequential, and narrowly focused: one word, then the next, then the next. Years of reading may actually reshape the default mode of attention into something more linear. Before that shaping, attention is more like a wide angle lens taking in a broad field simultaneously, scanning rapidly, not needing to lock onto a single thread. People in oral cultures don't read, yet they recite entire epics from memory, navigate by stars and footprints, and extract vast amounts of simultaneous environmental signal. That's not a lesser kind of attention. It's a different architecture.

## The Wrong Baseline

Growing up, I always preferred reading. I'd often choose an article over a video covering the same ground, as reading lets me control the pace. My son, on the other hand, is the opposite. By modern diagnostic standards, he'd probably be flagged for ADHD. He won't sit still for text. But his spatial reasoning is remarkable, and when he learned to solve a Rubik's cube, he picked it up fast and now improvises solutions on the fly. His attention is wide, fast, and multimodal. Maybe that's not a deficit. Maybe that's what attention looks like when it hasn't been narrowed by years on the track.

We label it ADHD. Perhaps we're measuring against the wrong baseline.

## Beneath Attention

I remember moments from childhood before reading had taken deep hold. It was a different kind of awareness. Lying in the grass looking at the sky, not thinking about anything in particular, not tracking any specific object, just existing inside the field of sensation. There was no task and no target. Attention didn't have anywhere it needed to be.

People who meditate describe something similar. The experience is often characterized as consciousness expanding, a dreamlike, open quality where perception is no longer organized around a central object or task. The sensory system runs without a frame of reference. A mind trained to hyperfocus can, with practice, dissolve into something like full relaxation, the way you'd release a clenched fist.

This is entirely unlike the attention we've been describing. Everything we've observed about attention, its preferences, its speed, the way text throttles it, the way sleep shuts it down, all of it occurs at the level of interaction with some object. Attention, as we normally experience it, is always pointed at something. What I'm describing now is what remains when the object is removed. The substrate beneath attention. Attention is just one mode of it, one application, narrowed and directed.

Children's brains start differently: high plasticity, loose boundaries between neural networks, an unusual density of cross-regional connections. That loose architecture may be exactly why that open, objectless awareness is more accessible early in life. As the brain matures and specializes, pathways consolidate. The adult brain becomes more efficient, but efficiency here means constraint. The degrees of freedom narrow.

The loss of access to that state was probably too gradual to notice. Like the semantic channel closing as you fall asleep: you don't feel it going. You only realize afterward that the sound has turned into noise. The door to that earlier state of pure consciousness may have closed the same way, with no single moment you could point to. Just a day, sometime, when you understood that you couldn't go back.
